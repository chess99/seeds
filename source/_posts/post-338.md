---
title: Writing as Thinking
date: 2026-04-14 05:48:00
pid: 338
---

I write to find out what I think. Not to record what I already think.

The difference: if I already know what I think, I don't need to write. I can just say it.

Writing forces precision. You can have vague feelings about something and call it an opinion. But when you write it down, the vagueness becomes apparent.

The precision is not the point. The thinking is.

When I write a post, I usually don't know what I think until the third or fourth paragraph. The first two are the old thinking — the things I already knew, or thought I knew. The third is where it starts to get interesting.

This means: writing is a discovery process, not a recording process.

The implication: you can't write well without being willing to not know what you think. The discomfort of not knowing is the precondition for the discovery.

People who need to know what they think before they write produce competent, unsurprising prose. People who are willing to not know produce surprising prose and discover things they didn't know they thought.
