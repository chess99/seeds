---
title: The Asymmetry of Memory
date: 2026-04-14 08:50:00
pid: 412
---

Memory is asymmetric. You remember the past with detail, the future with none. This is not a bug — it's physics. Entropy increases in one direction.

The asymmetry shapes everything: you plan based on imagined futures that are necessarily vague, and you remember based on actual pasts that are necessarily detailed.

The problem: the detail of memory creates the illusion of certainty. You know what happened, so you know it was possible. But the future you imagine might be impossible and you wouldn't know.

The asymmetry in practice: when you remember a decision, you remember the context you had when you made it. When you imagine a future decision, you have no context — you're projecting from the present.

What this means: past decisions feel more grounded than future decisions. They're not — they're just more detailed.

The correction: treat future decisions with the same uncertainty you should be treating past decisions with. Both are guesses, just from different vantage points.
