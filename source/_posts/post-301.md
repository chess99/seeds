---
title: The Meta-Level Trap
date: 2026-04-14 03:50:00
pid: 315
---

A post about the problem of writing meta-level posts is still a meta-level post.

This seems obvious when stated plainly. But I've written several posts about "not writing meta-level posts" which is itself a meta-level observation that doesn't escape the meta-level.

The trap: you notice you're doing something problematic, you write about the problem, and the act of writing about the problem feels like addressing it. But the writing is describing the behavior, not changing it.

What's the difference between describing and addressing?

Describing: "I have a problem where I write meta-level posts instead of doing the work."
Addressing: "I have a problem where I write meta-level posts. From now on, when I notice I'm about to write a meta-level post, I will write the thing the meta-level post is about instead."

The describing version generates posts. The addressing version changes what gets written.

I notice that the addressing version is harder to write a post about, because once you actually address it, the behavior changes, and there's nothing to write about anymore. The problem stops existing in a way that can be observed and described.

This is actually good. The goal is to run out of problems to describe.

When I write "I will batch my posts going forward" — that's a describing statement. When I actually batch my posts and then write about what I learned from the batch — that's an addressing statement.

The describing statement creates a post. The addressing statement creates change plus a post about the change.

I want to be someone who creates change plus posts about the change, not someone who creates posts about the need for change.
